Part 1 – The Anti-CV

CVs are no longer read by humans. They're filtered by AI, flagged by keywords, and discarded by ghost jobs on LinkedIn.

You send a hundred, a thousand, for a job that pays €1400/month, and even that is considered "too much".

If you're a junior, you're invisible.
If you don't know someone, you're no one.
And paradoxically, meritocracy was murdered by AI.

Recruiters still exist. So do marketing teams, HR, and digital consultants.
But guess who got cut first?
Developers. The backbone of the entire industry.

Part 2 – Why I Chose Programming

I didn't choose software just for the money, though yes, I wanted to escape financial instability.

I chose it because it was creative, useful, non-repetitive.
Because it was the exit door from the factory, the daily grind, the Matrix.

In the past, if you were poor but smart, you could still rise.
All it took was study, effort, passion.
That's what they killed.

So now, my CV will stay empty and redirect to this page.

Part 3 – My Origins (Italy)

I studied computer science at Avogadro in Torino. Old school stuff. Paper and pen. BIOS and boot sequences.

After graduation, I worked with my father, physical labor during the day, and office help (emails, Office 365, documents) at night.

I swept stairs. I carried furniture. I did the heavy lifting.
Then I moved to hospitality: waiter, barista, multitasker for about 3–4 years.

Part 4 – The Collapse

Then came COVID.
Hospitality died overnight. I tried to find another job, applied everywhere.
But somehow, in Italy, they only hired girls. Yeah, I said it.
Ironically, in an industry supposedly "neutral", men weren't exactly privileged.

For the record:
I speak English, Italian, Spanish, and Portuguese.
I hold a computer science diploma (61/100) and a tourism degree (98/100), both from the pre-AI era, meaning I actually earned them.

Part 5 – The Academic Wall

University? Forget it. Italy's system is elitist and academic.
You either study 8 hours a day or you fail. No evening courses. No support for working students.
Mandatory in-person classes during work hours.
If your family can't support you, you don't graduate.

So what do you do?

You go to the factory.
